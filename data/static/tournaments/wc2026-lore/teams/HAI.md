# Haiti (HAI)

## Tournament History
2nd World Cup appearance (1974, 2026). The 1974 squad in West Germany
went out in the group stage but scored a famous goal through
Emmanuel Sanon that briefly silenced Italy. Fifty-two years without
a World Cup. This qualification is miraculous by any measure: a
nation of 11 million people, devastated by the 2010 earthquake, by
political instability, by gang violence — fielding a squad at the
2026 World Cup. The story of getting here dwarfs the results on
the pitch.

## Current Squad
Coach: Sebastien Migne (FRA). French coach who never set foot in
Haiti during the entire qualifying campaign due to the country's
security crisis — managed the team entirely remotely. The architect
of Haiti's qualification
campaign. Pragmatic, defensive-minded, built around a hard-to-beat
structure.
Assistant: Sebastien Rabault (FRA).

System: 4-4-2 / 4-5-1. Compact, organized, limits space. Not here
to play pretty football — here to compete, make it difficult, and
find moments of quality from diaspora-connected players who mostly
ply their trade in Ligue 2, MLS, and lower European leagues.

GK: Johny Placide (#1, SC Bastia) — the veteran keeper, born
1988-01-29, experienced Ligue 2 goalkeeper. The oldest player
in the squad.

Key players: Jean-Ricner Bellegarde (#10, Wolverhampton) — the
number 10, plays in the Premier League, the squad's most prominent
European club player. The creative hub when Haiti have the ball.
Derrick Etienne Jr. (#7, Toronto FC) — forward, Canadian-Haitian,
link to the North American roots of this squad. Wilson Isidor (#18,
Sunderland) — forward, most likely to score. Duckens Nazon (#9,
Esteghlal) — experienced striker, plays in Iran. Frantzdy Pierrot
(#20, Caykur Rizespor) — forward, Turkish league.

Midfield: Carl Sainte (#6, El Paso Locomotive, USA) — energetic
midfielder. Danley Jean Jacques (#17, Philadelphia Union) — MLS
experience. Jean-Kevin Duverne (#22, KAA Gent) — Belgian league.

Defence: Hannes Delcroix (#5, FC Lugano) — Swiss-based centre-back.
Martin Experience (#8, AS Nancy) — the name is genuine, the
centre-back plays in France. Carlens Arcus (#2, Angers).
Ricardo Ade (#4, LDU Quito) — veteran centre-back.

Young: Keeto Thermoncy (#3, BSC Young Boys, born 2006-03-29) —
20 years old, youngest in the squad. Woodensky Pierre (#26,
Violette AC — Haiti domestic league, born 2004-12-30) — the
only player competing domestically in Haiti.

The MLS/CONCACAF backbone: Sainte (El Paso), Jean Jacques
(Philadelphia), Etienne Jr. (Toronto), Leverton Pierre (#14,
Colorado Springs), Duke Lacroix (#13, Colorado Springs) — five
players from North American leagues. The squad's geography tells
the story of the Haitian diaspora.

## Group C Results
- HAI 0-1 SCO (Matchday 1) — McGinn 28'. Haiti competed well for
  the first half hour before McGinn's goal. Defended deep and
  organized throughout, limited Scotland to one goal. A respectable
  if losing performance against a European qualifier.
- BRA 3-0 HAI (Matchday 2) — M. Cunha 23' 36', Vinicius Jr 45+3'.
  Outclassed by Brazil's quality. The 3-0 scoreline was the most
  accurate description of the gap in class. Haiti had no answer for
  Cunha's movement and Vinicius's directness. No goals conceded in
  the first 22 minutes showed some organization.

0 pts, GD -4. Eliminated. MD3 vs Morocco is the final match —
a chance to compete for pride and the historic significance of
their first ever World Cup win.

## Storylines
- First World Cup since 1974: fifty-two years. The qualification
  campaign navigated gang violence in Port-au-Prince, political
  crisis, natural disasters, and regional qualifiers against CONCACAF
  opposition with far greater resources. The story of getting here
  is the story.
- The diaspora squad: this is a squad of Haitian-heritage players
  based in France, Switzerland, Belgium, USA, Canada, the Netherlands.
  Woodensky Pierre (#26) is the only player competing in Haiti itself.
  The country's football infrastructure is rebuilt through its
  international community.
- Bellegarde's Premier League quality: the Wolverhampton midfielder
  is Haiti's most prominent club player. His presence in the squad
  represents the peak of Haitian football development — a player
  competing weekly in England's top division.
- Migne's achievement: a French coach who could have taken a
  higher-profile national team job chose Haiti. The qualification
  campaign under his tenure is one of the more remarkable
  achievements in CONCACAF football this cycle.
- 0-4 in two matches but not disgraced: Brazil's 3-0 could have
  been more. Scotland's 1-0 was tight for long periods. Haiti
  competed within their means. The gap to the top of world football
  is real but not embarrassing.

## Observed Patterns
- Defensive organization is the primary tactical identity — the
  team sits in a compact shape and forces opponents to find
  solutions rather than offering them space
- Bellegarde as the single creative outlet: when Haiti do get
  the ball, everything channels through the #10
- Limited athletic gap against Scotland but exposed against
  Brazil's individual quality — Cunha and Vinicius are in a
  different tier
- Placide's experience behind the goal has been crucial for
  keeping the scores respectable; his distribution is limited
  but his shot-stopping is reliable
- Set piece defending has been solid across both matches —
  goals conceded have come from open play quality, not dead balls
