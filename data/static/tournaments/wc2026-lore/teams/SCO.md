# Scotland (SCO)

## Tournament History
9th World Cup appearance — but the first since 1998 in France.
Twenty-eight years. Scotland are the most frustrated nation in World
Cup history: qualified nine times but have never passed the group
stage. They came agonizingly close in 1974, 1978, 1982 (goal
difference), 1990. The 2026 appearance ends the longest absence
(1998-2026) in their history. The parallel with Austria — another
European nation ending a long World Cup drought — is noted by
commentators. The weight of 28 years of near-misses is visible in
every match.

## Current Squad
Coach: Steve Clark (SCO). A journeyman Scottish manager whose
name has been mangled in broadcast transcriptions as "Stephen Clark"
— he is confirmed as Steve Clarke. Long career at Kilmarnock,
West Brom, and the national team since 2019. Steady, organized,
cautious. Brought Scotland to their first major tournament (Euro 2020)
and now their first World Cup in 28 years.
Assistants: Andrew Hughes (SCO), Alan Irvine (SCO — experienced
Premier League experience as manager), Steven Naismith (SCO — former
international, now youth coach).

System: 3-5-2 / 4-3-3. Clark uses a back three when defending
deep, widening to a high line of five. McGinn, McTominay, and
Christie as the midfield three. Robertson bombing forward from
left wing-back.

GK: Angus Gunn (#1, Nottingham Forest) — the primary keeper.
Liam Kelly (#12, Rangers) backup. Craig Gordon (#21, Hearts, born
1982-12-31) — 43 years old, the oldest player at the entire 2026
World Cup across all 48 squads. Former British record GK transfer
(£9m to Sunderland, 2007). Survived a career-threatening double
leg-break. His inclusion even as third keeper is one
of the tournament's age curiosities.

The engine room: Scott McTominay (#4, Napoli) — the Serie A
midfielder, physical, box-to-box. John McGinn (#7, Aston Villa) —
the captain and scorer. McGinn's 28th-minute winner vs Haiti
was Scotland's first World Cup goal since 1998. Andy Robertson (#3,
Liverpool) — the left wing-back or fullback, one of the world's
best at his position when fit, born 1994-03-11. Kieran Tierney (#6,
Celtic) — experienced left-back option.

Attackers: Ché Adams (#10, Torino) — the striker, 12 international
goals. Lyndon Dykes (#9, Charlton) — the aerial threat. Ross
Stewart (#14, Southampton) — physical forward alternative.
Lawrence Shankland (#20, Hearts) — domestic-based striker.
Ryan Christie (#11, Bournemouth) — the winger/midfielder hybrid.

Young talent: Aaron Hickey (#2, Brentford, born 2002-06-10) —
24-year-old right back, one of the best in England's top flight.
Tyler Fletcher (#8, Manchester United, born 2007-03-19) — just
19 years old, the youngest in the squad. Ben Gannon-Doak (#17,
Bournemouth, born 2005-11-11) — 20 years old. Nathan Patterson
(#22, Everton, born 2001-10-16). Findlay Curtis (#25, Kilmarnock,
born 2006-06-09) — 20 years old.

Veterans: Grant Hanley (#5, Hibernian, born 1991-11-20), Kenny
McLean (#23, Norwich, born 1992-01-08) — both 34-35, the
experienced spine.

## Group C Results
- HAI 0-1 SCO (Matchday 1) — McGinn 28'. The goal that ended
  28 years of World Cup absence. McGinn's composed finish in the
  28th minute was Scotland's first World Cup goal since Morocco 1998.
  A professional win against a limited opponent, but the emotional
  significance was enormous.
- SCO 0-1 MAR (Matchday 2) — Saibari 2'. A 2nd-minute Saibari
  goal defined the match. Scotland had chances but couldn't level.
  The loss to Morocco was a reality check — the improvement needed
  to compete with genuinely elite opposition was exposed.

3 pts, GD 0. Third in the group. MD3 vs Brazil: a win combined
with Morocco losing to Haiti would put Scotland through. A long
shot, but the scenario exists.

## Storylines
- The 28-year wait ends: McGinn's goal vs Haiti was the moment
  28 million Scots exhaled simultaneously. Scotland's return to
  the World Cup stage after the 1998 absence is a story written
  over six managers and multiple failed qualifying campaigns. Steve
  Clarke delivered what his predecessors couldn't.
- McGinn the goal-scorer: the Aston Villa midfielder captained
  Scotland to their first World Cup win since 1996 (when they beat
  Switzerland — coincidentally also in Group B of this tournament's
  counterpart group). His goal was composed, low, into the corner.
  The captain's moment.
- Robertson's importance: when Robertson plays at his Liverpool best,
  Scotland's attacking width and crossing quality transforms. He is
  the player who lifts the team's ceiling. Managing his fitness
  through a tournament is Steve Clarke's most important tactical task.
- Tyler Fletcher at 19: a Manchester United teenager in the Scotland
  squad at a World Cup. Born 2007. The future of Scottish football
  in the building.
- Craig Gordon at 43: the Hearts goalkeeper born in 1982 is in
  the squad as third keeper. He has played for Scotland since 2004.
  Twenty-two years of service, still in a World Cup squad at 43.

## Observed Patterns
- Organized defensively — Scotland are hard to break down and
  McGinn's discipline off the ball is the key to their shape
- Set piece threat: McGinn, McTominay, and Robertson all deliver
  quality from dead balls; Scotland's best chances often come
  from restarts
- Vulnerable to early goals against quality opponents — Saibari
  scored in the 2nd minute vs Morocco and Scotland spent 88
  minutes chasing a game they never controlled
- McTominay as the physical engine: his Serie A development has
  added tactical intelligence to his athleticism; he reads the
  game better than at any point in his career
- Robertson's overlapping runs are Scotland's primary attacking
  source — if he's contained or out of form, the attack becomes
  one-dimensional through central areas
