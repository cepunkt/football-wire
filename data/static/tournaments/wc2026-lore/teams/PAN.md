# Panama (PAN)

## Tournament History
2nd World Cup appearance. 2018 Russia was the first — eliminated
in the group stage (lost to Belgium 3-0, England 6-1, Tunisia 2-1).
The nation celebrated just qualifying in 2018 as if they'd won the
tournament. Roman Torres's qualification goal in 2017 remains
one of CONCACAF's defining moments. Now back for the second time.
A country of four million people, the canal as its identity, and
football as an increasingly serious national project.

## Current Squad
Coach: Thomas Christiansen (DEN/ESP). Danish passport, Spanish-
raised, former Barcelona B and La Liga player. Coached Cyprus,
the Philippines, and now Panama. The cosmopolitan coaching appointment
that fits Panama's ambition without having obvious local alternatives.
Assistants: Javier Sanchez, Daniel Blanco.

System: 4-4-2 / 4-2-3-1. Organized, hard to beat, physical.
CONCACAF football's trademark pragmatism. Most players based in
Central American and Mexican leagues with some European-based
outliers. This is not a technically sophisticated squad — it is
a well-drilled, competitive unit.

GK: Luis Mejia (#1, Club Nacional) — Uruguayan-based Panamanian
keeper. First choice. Orlando Mosquera (#22, Al Fayha) —
the experienced backup.

Defence: Michael Amir Murillo (#23, Besiktas) — right-back at
Besiktas in Turkey, the most globally prominent Panamanian defender.
Fidel Escobar (#4, Deportivo Saprissa) — CB. Jose Cordoba (#3,
Norwich City) — born June 2001, CB option. Eric Davis (#15,
CD Plaza Amador) — born 1991, the veteran defensive anchor.
Roderick Miller (#25, Turan Tovuz) — the experienced CB.
Andres Andrade (#16, LASK Linz) — born 1998, Austria-based option.

Midfield: Adalberto Carrasquilla (#8, Pumas UNAM) — the creative
centre. Aníbal Godoy (#20, San Diego FC) — born 1990, 36. The
veteran holding midfielder, the elder statesman of the squad.
Alberto Quintero (#19, CD Plaza Amador) — born December 1987, 38.
Still in the squad at 38. Ismael Diaz (#10, Club Leon) — the 10,
the most technically gifted. Yoel Barcenas (#11, Mazatlan) — the
winger with Mexican league experience. Cristian Martinez (#6,
Hapoel Kiryat Shmona) — Israeli league-based midfielder.

Attack: Jose Fajardo (#17, Universidad Católica Ecuador) — the
senior striker option. Cecilio Waterman (#18, Universidad de
Concepcion) — born 1991, the target man. Tomas Rodriguez (#9,
Deportivo Saprissa) — the domestic forward option. Azarias Londono
(#24, Universidad Católica Ecuador) — born June 2001, 25.

The Central America connection: Rodriguez and Fajardo both at
Deportivo Saprissa, the Costa Rican giants. Panama's footballers
operate across CONCACAF's regional ecosystem.

## Group L Results
- GHA 1-0 PAN (Matchday 1) — Ghana's Caleb Yirenkyi scored at
  90+5'. Panama competed, worked hard, pushed for the opener
  throughout, and conceded to a 20-year-old's late header in
  the 95th minute. The margin was razor-thin and the timing was
  brutal. Panama hadn't conceded; they were punished in injury time.

0 pts. A match they might have drawn on another day. A 95th-minute

## Storylines
- Back for the second time: 2018 Russia was the dream. 2026 USA/
  Canada/Mexico is the attempt to prove they belong. The second
  World Cup is always harder — opponents have tape, know what you
  do, arrive prepared for you. Panama's first appearance was
  celebration. This one is examination.
- Godoy at 36: Anibal Godoy has been the anchor of Panama's midfield
  for over a decade. Born 1990, playing in MLS at San Diego FC.
  The player who bridged the 2018 generation and this one.
- Quintero at 38: Alberto Quintero was born in December 1987. He
  is in the World Cup squad at 38. Still running, still contributing.
  The veteran who refuses to go quietly.
- The 95th-minute heartbreak: Panama were competitive. They pushed.
  And then a 20-year-old Ghanaian who plays in Denmark scored in the
  last touch of the game. Football's cruelty delivered in precise
  timing.
- Christiansen's CONCACAF project: a Danish-Spanish coach who has
  worked in the Philippines and Cyprus brought to lead Panama. The
  tactical internationalism of national football at this level.

## Observed Patterns
- Hard to break down — Ghana required until the 95th minute to find
  a goal — defensive organization is the primary asset
- Physical, direct, CONCACAF-style football that discomforts
  technically superior opponents
- Limited attacking quality in the final third — opportunities
  created but insufficient conversion threat
- Experienced central midfield (Godoy, Quintero) provides
  structure and leadership but not pace
- Murillo's right-back position is an attacking threat — one of
  the squad's more technically complete players
