# Iraq (IRQ)

## Tournament History
2nd World Cup ever. Only previous appearance: 1986 in Mexico (group stage,
three defeats, 1 goal, 4 conceded). The 40-year gap is the longest return
in this tournament. Iraq qualified through the AFC's brutal third-round
process, ending a decades-long absence. Historical moment for a football
nation that has survived everything.

## Current Squad
Coach: Graham Arnold (AUS). Appointed May 2025. Managed Australia for
6 years (longest-serving Socceroos coach) including the 2022 WC R16 run,
resigned Sep 2024. Now managing Iraq — one of the most unusual coaching
pivots in WC history. Second World Cup, different flag on his chest.
Assistants: Rene Meulensteen (NED) — former Manchester United first-team
coach under Sir Alex Ferguson, was in Fergie's technical staff for multiple
Premier League titles. Robert Stanton.

System: Organised defensive structure with direct counter-attacking. Arnold's
template — hard to beat, strike on transitions — adapted for Iraqi personnel.
Meulensteen brings top-level European coaching methodology to the staff.

GK: Fahad Talib (#1, Al Talaba) — domestic-based starter. Jalal Hassan
(#12, Al Zawra'a) and Ahmed Basil (#22, Al Shorta) backups. Predominantly
Iraqi league keepers.

Defence: Rebin Sulaka (#2, Port FC Thailand) born 1992 — most experienced
defender. Hussein Ali (#3, Pogoń Szczecin) born 2002 — Poland-based youth.
Zaid Tahseen (#4, Pakhtakor) born 2001. Akam Hashim (#5, Al Zawra'a) born
1998. Manaf Younis (#6, Al Shorta). Merchas Doski (#23, Viktoria Plzeň) —
Czech-based CB. Frans Putros (#26, Persib Bandung Indonesia) born 1993 —
the squad's most travelled defender.

Midfield: Zidane Iqbal (#14, FC Utrecht) — born 2003, Manchester United
academy product, his name tells his story. Named after Zinedine Zidane by
his British-Iraqi parents. Kevin Yakob (#19, Aarhus GF) born 2000. Ibrahim
Bayesh (#8, Al Dhafra UAE). Amir Al-Ammari (#16, KS Cracovia) born 1997.
Aimar Sher (#20, Sarpsborg 08) born 2002 — Norwegian league. Youssef Amyn
(#7, AEK Larnaca) born 2003. Zaid Ismail (#24, Al Talaba).

Attack: Aymen Hussein (#18, Al Karma) — the striker, scored vs Norway at
39'. Born 1996, domestic-based. Ali Al-Hamadi (#9, Luton Town) born 2002 —
the English league link. Ahmed Qasem (#11, Nashville SC) born 2003 — MLS-
based. Ali Jasim (#17, Al Najmah) born 2004. Marko Farji (#21, Venezia)
born 2004 — Italian league. Mohanad Ali (#10, Dibba FC UAE).

## Storylines
- Graham Arnold: Australian coach, Iraqi team, World Cup stage. He took
  the Socceroos to their best World Cup in two decades and then crossed
  football's strangest career boundary. Iraq trusted his methodology.
  Whether it works is a different question.
- Meulensteen under the Fergie shadow: Rene Meulensteen was on Sir Alex
  Ferguson's staff at Manchester United. Now he's the assistant coach of
  Iraq at the World Cup. Football's global dispersal of expertise in one
  coaching appointment.
- Zidane Iqbal: named after Zinedine Zidane, born to British-Iraqi parents,
  developed at Manchester United's academy, now playing for Iraq. The
  biography writes the narrative.
- Hussein's goal: Aymen Hussein scoring against Norway meant Iraq aren't
  just passengers. A domestic-league striker representing a football
  nation at the World Cup — that goal matters beyond the scoreline.
- The 40-year return: from 1986 Mexico to 2026 North America — 40 years
  in the wilderness. The players on this pitch were born long after Iraq's
  last appearance.

## Observed Patterns
- Organised but outgunned: showed defensive structure vs Norway before
  Haaland's quality overwhelmed them
- Hussein's positional intelligence: his goal showed timing and composure
  inside the box, not just work rate
- Hybrid squad profile: Iraqi domestic players alongside European-based
  youth (Iqbal, Ali, Qasem, Farji, Yakob) — Arnold trying to blend systems
- Counter-attack potential: the concession structure vs Norway showed
  defensive shape until individual errors opened gaps
- OG in stoppage time: the 90+6' own goal by Hussein came from sustained
  Norwegian pressure — fitness and concentration late in games is a
  structural concern
