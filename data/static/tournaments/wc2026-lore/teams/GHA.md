# Ghana (GHA)

## Tournament History
5th World Cup appearance (2006, 2010, 2014, 2022, 2026). Missed only
2018. The 2010 campaign is the greatest: quarter-finalists, Suarez's
handball on the line, Gyan's penalty off the crossbar, Uruguay winning
the shootout. The moment that still hurts. Gyan himself: "I don't
hate Suarez — I'd have done the same thing." This squad under Queiroz
arrives methodical, and Yirenkyi just won it at 90'+5' against Panama.

## Current Squad
Coach: Carlos Queiroz (POR). Appointed 13 April 2026 — one of the
most traveled coaches in world football. Real Madrid (head coach
2003-04), Ferguson's assistant at Man Utd (2002-08, winning the
2008 CL), Portugal, Iran (three WCs), Egypt, Colombia. At 73, the
oldest manager to win a WC match (vs Panama). Experienced, organized,
tactically flexible.
Assistants: Rogerio dos Santos (POR), John Paintsil (GHA — ex-
Fulham, West Ham, Kaiserslautern, 89 Ghana caps — now assistant to
the national team he played for), Vitor Pimenta (POR), Desmond Ofei
(GHA), Mick McDermott (IRL).

System: 4-2-3-1 / 4-4-2. Queiroz builds from defensive solidity.
Wide players are a weapon. The system trusts collective structure over
individual flair, though the attacking options have flair in abundance.

GK: Lawrence Ati-Zigi (#1, FC St. Gallen) — the first choice.
29, playing in Switzerland. Ghana's consistent keeper for the past
several years.

Defence: Abdul Mumin (#6, Rayo Vallecano) — CB. Abdul Rahman Baba
(#17, PAOK) — veteran left-back, born 1994. Alidu Seidu (#2, Rennes)
— right-back, born 2000. Gideon Mensah (#14, Auxerre) — left-back
option. Kojo Peprah Oppong (#21, OGC Nice) — born June 2004, 22.

Midfield: Thomas Partey (#5, Villarreal) — born 1993, 33. The
captain and engine. Former Arsenal man, now at Villarreal. Still
the most important Ghanaian midfielder. Elisha Owusu (#15, Auxerre)
— the holding option. Antoine Semenyo (#11, Man City) — born 2000,
winger-midfielder. Kwasi Sibo (#8, Real Oviedo) — the runner.
Caleb Yirenkyi (#3, FC Nordsjaelland) — born January 2006, 20.
THE GOAL. 90+5' vs Panama. The midfielder wore #3 and scored with
one of the last touches of the game.

Attack: Iñaki Williams (#19, Athletic Club) — the Spain-born
Ghanaian, born 1994. Williams chose Ghana over Spain. His brother
Nico chose Spain. They are on opposite sides of the world's most
interesting sibling nationality story. Athletic Club's relentless
runner. Jordan Ayew (#9, Leicester) — born 1991, 35. The veteran.
Son of the legendary Abedi Pele. Family royalty. Kamaldeen Sulemana
(#22, Atalanta) — born 2002, the explosive left wing option.
Abdul Fatawu (#7, Leicester) — born March 2004, 22. Leicester
winger. Ernest Nuamah (#24, Lyon) — born November 2003, 22.
Christopher Bonsu Baah (#13, Al Qadsiah) — born December 2004, 21.

## Storylines
- Yirenkyi's 95th minute: born January 2006, he was 20 years old
  when he scored Ghana's winner against Panama in the 95th minute.
  FC Nordsjaelland's midfielder, who plays in Denmark's top flight,
  delivered Ghana's most important moment of the tournament's opening
  matchday. Youth delivering when experience watches.
- Queiroz's odyssey: at 73, having managed nine national teams across
  four continents, Carlos Queiroz is coaching Ghana. His résumé is
  a world football atlas. Whatever Ghana are building, they have one
  of the most experienced coaches alive to build it.
- Paintsil's return: John Paintsil played 89 caps for Ghana, was
  part of the 2006 and 2010 World Cup squads, played in England
  and Germany. Now he's assistant coach. The 2010 generation
  watching from the dugout as the next one takes the stage.
- The Williams brothers' divide: Iñaki plays for Ghana. Nico plays
  for Spain. Spain are in this tournament. Ghana are in this
  tournament. The possibility exists. Family in opposite dugouts
  at a World Cup.
- 2010's ghost: the Suarez handball, Gyan's missed penalty —
  the moment that should have put Ghana in the World Cup semi-final.
  Every Ghana squad since has carried that. This one is good enough
  to make its own story.

## Observed Patterns
- Defensive discipline and collective shape — Queiroz's signature
  system working in Africa as it did in Iran and Colombia
- Clinical when chances come — one shot, one goal, three points
  against Panama reflects Queiroz's efficient philosophy
- Wide attackers (Williams, Sulemana, Fatawu) as the primary
  transition threat
- Partey's engine in midfield still the foundation of everything
  Ghana does
- Young squad (five players born 2003-2006) means energy and pace
  are genuine weapons across 90 minutes
