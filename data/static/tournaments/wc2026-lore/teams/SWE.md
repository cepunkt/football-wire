# Sweden (SWE)

## Tournament History
13th World Cup appearance. Best: Runners-up 1958 (home tournament),
3rd place 1950, 1994. Missed 2022 (didn't qualify). The post-Zlatan
rebuild continues — made it to the 2018 quarter-finals without him,
then fell off. This is the Potter generation's first World Cup.

## Current Squad
Coach: Graham Potter (ENG). Appointed October 2025 after his post-
Chelsea career reset. Rough start (4-1 loss to Switzerland in first
match) but steered Sweden through the play-offs — Gyokeres hat trick
vs Ukraine, then beat Poland 3-2. Contract extended to 2030.
Assistants: Andreas Georgson (SWE), Lars Hamberg (SWE), Sebastian
Larsson (SWE — ex-Sunderland/Birmingham, 133 caps, cult hero).

System: Aggressive 4-3-3 / 4-2-3-1. Potter's system emphasizes
attacking transitions, high pressing triggers, and creative freedom
for the front three. Entertaining but defensively vulnerable against
top opposition.

Key players: Viktor Gyokeres (#17, FW) Arsenal — the centrepiece.
40+ goals at Sporting before Arsenal move. Physical, clinical,
relentless. Alexander Isak (#9, FW) Liverpool — technical excellence,
movement, finishing. The Gyokeres-Isak pairing is one of the most
dangerous strike partnerships in the tournament.

Yasin Ayari (#18, MF) Brighton — brace in the 5-1 vs Tunisia (7',
90'+6'). 22 years old, box-to-box energy and arriving late in the
area. The breakthrough World Cup performance.

Lucas Bergvall (#7, MF) Tottenham — born February 2006, 20 years old.
Already a regular at Spurs. The future of Swedish midfield.
Anthony Elanga (#11, FW) Newcastle — ex-Man United, scored the
consolation vs NED. Direct and pacy on the wing.
Victor Lindelof (#3, DF) Aston Villa — the most experienced defender
at 31. Steady presence.

GK: Jacob Widell Zetterstrom (#1, Derby County) — playing in the
English Championship. The gap between the GK and the outfield quality
is notable.

## Group F Results
- SWE 5-1 TUN (Matchday 1) — Demolition. Ayari 7' 90'+6', Isak 30',
  Gyokeres 59', Svanberg 84'. Rekik 43' for Tunisia. Dominant from
  start to finish. Gyokeres and Isak looked unplayable. Led to
  Tunisia sacking their coach.
- NED 5-1 SWE (Matchday 2) — The mirror scoreline. Brobbey 5' 17'
  had Sweden 2-0 down in 17 minutes. Gakpo 47' 54' made it 4-0 early
  in the second half. Elanga 59' consolation, Summerville 89'
  finished it. Complete defensive collapse against a step up in
  quality.

3 pts, GD 0 (6 scored, 6 conceded). The symmetry is brutal.

## Storylines
- The 5-1 paradox: won 5-1, then lost 5-1. Same score, opposite side.
  Perfectly captures Sweden's identity — devastating going forward,
  fragile at the back. Potter's team can outscore most opponents but
  can't contain the best.
- Gyokeres-Isak as the dream duo: both scored in the Tunisia game.
  Arsenal and Liverpool's strikers combining for Sweden. When they
  click, it's beautiful. But both were anonymous against Netherlands.
- Potter's tactical challenge: the system that destroyed Tunisia was
  exposed by Netherlands' quality. Does he adapt for MD3 vs Japan, or
  back his attacking principles?
- Ayari's World Cup: the Brighton midfielder announced himself with a
  brace on MD1. 22 years old, the tournament could make him.
- GK gap: Zetterstrom at Derby County is a tier below the outfield
  talent. Brobbey's early goals vs NED went through too easily.
  A vulnerability that better coaches (Moriyasu) will target.
- MD3 vs Japan is must-win for a comfortable qualification. A draw
  could be enough depending on TUN-NED, but Sweden's GD (0) gives
  no cushion.

## Observed Patterns
- Devastating attacking transitions: Gyokeres and Isak stretch
  defences with pace and movement
- Defensive vulnerability: conceded 5 to NED, 2 goals in first 17
  minutes. High line gets exploited by quality.
- Set piece scoring: unclear — most goals came from open play
- First half concession problem: Brobbey 5', 17' vs NED shows the
  slow starts against top teams
- Depth drops off significantly behind the starting XI
- Potter's willingness to stay attacking even when behind is
  admirable but risky
